## The Medical PEEL Paragraph Structure

Every manuscript body paragraph (except abstract) follows this four-element structure. Minimum 5 sentences per paragraph; target 6–7.

```
P — Point        (1 sentence): State the clinical observation or claim
E — Evidence     (1–2 sentences): Present the specific data, p-value, or citation
E — Elaboration  (2–3 sentences): Interpret the finding — why it matters mechanistically/clinically
L — Link         (1 sentence): Connect to the next paragraph or section theme
```

### Worked Example — Results paragraph

**Point:** Asciminib demonstrated superior molecular response rates compared with bosutinib at 96 weeks of follow-up.

**Evidence (E1):** In the ASCEMBL trial, major molecular response was achieved in 29.3% of patients receiving asciminib versus 13.2% of those receiving bosutinib at 96 weeks (rate difference, 16.0 percentage points; 95% CI, 6.0–26.0; p=0.002) [1].

**Elaboration (E2):** The magnitude of this difference increased with longer follow-up, suggesting durable benefit rather than a transient early advantage. Mechanistically, this sustained superiority likely reflects asciminib's unique allosteric binding at the myristoyl pocket of BCR-ABL1, which confers activity against the majority of kinase domain mutations that emerge during TKI therapy and reduce drug binding at the ATP-binding site [2].

**Elaboration (E3):** The clinical significance of deeper molecular responses is well established; sustained MR4 or MR4.5 for a minimum of two years is considered a prerequisite for treatment-free remission attempts under current European LeukemiaNet recommendations [3].

**Link:** The safety profile accompanying these superior efficacy outcomes is therefore relevant to assessing the feasibility of long-term treatment adherence necessary for TFR candidacy.

> **Result:** 6-sentence paragraph, ~140 words. Compare with a 2-sentence AI-typical paragraph (~35 words).

## Section Blueprints

### Abstract (Systematic Review, ~240 words)

Each part must be fully developed — no placeholder sentences.

**Background (2–3 sentences, ~50 words):**
- Sentence 1: Disease burden + unmet need
- Sentence 2: Current treatment standard + limitation
- Sentence 3: The intervention and why it warrants review

**Methods (2–3 sentences, ~55 words):**
- Sentence 1: Design, databases searched, date range
- Sentence 2: Eligibility criteria (population, intervention, comparator, outcomes)
- Sentence 3: Primary and secondary endpoints

**Results (4–5 sentences, ~85 words):**
- Sentence 1: Studies screened → included (PRISMA numbers)
- Sentence 2: Primary outcome with absolute value and confidence interval
- Sentence 3: Key secondary outcome with specific data
- Sentence 4: Safety signal — grade ≥3 AE rate
- Sentence 5: Any subgroup or heterogeneity finding

**Conclusions (2–3 sentences, ~50 words):**
- Sentence 1: Restate the key finding in clinical terms
- Sentence 2: Clinical implication for practice
- Sentence 3: Direction for future research

### Introduction (Systematic Review, 4 paragraphs, ~750 words)

**Paragraph 1 — Disease burden and pathobiology (~160 words):**
Open with epidemiology (incidence/prevalence). Describe the molecular/cytogenetic basis (Philadelphia chromosome, BCR::ABL1 fusion, mechanism of oncogenesis). Specify clinical course (chronic, accelerated, blast phase progression). Include mortality/survival context before the TKI era versus after. End with: the molecular markers now guiding treatment decisions.

**Paragraph 2 — Treatment landscape and historical evolution (~170 words):**
Introduce imatinib as the first-in-class agent (year, mechanism, landmark trial result). Follow with second-generation TKIs — dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib — improvements over imatinib, key trial data (CCyR, MMR rates). Introduce third-generation TKIs and the specific problem of T315I resistance. End with: unmet need for patients failing ≥2 prior lines.

**Paragraph 3 — The intervention under review (~180 words):**
Introduce the intervention by mechanism first, not just name. Describe the structural/pharmacological distinctiveness (e.g., allosteric vs. ATP-competitive for asciminib). Summarize the pivotal trial(s) with specific efficacy numbers. Note any regulatory approvals and the patient populations covered. End with: the case for systematic synthesis of all available evidence.

**Paragraph 4 — Rationale for this review + objectives (~120 words):**
State the specific evidence gap (no prior comprehensive systematic review, conflicting results across trials, etc.). State the PRISMA-compliant design. State the primary and secondary objectives as complete prose sentences — not as a bulleted list. End with the PICO elements woven into a single declarative sentence ("This review evaluated the efficacy and safety of [intervention] compared with [comparator] in adults with [disease], with major molecular response at 12 months as the primary outcome.")

### Methods Prose Rules (No Bullet Lists)

ALL methods content must be written as flowing prose paragraphs. The following subsections MUST be prose:

**Eligibility Criteria (~150 words):**
> "We included randomized controlled trials and prospective cohort studies enrolling adults aged 18 years or older with Philadelphia chromosome-positive chronic myeloid leukemia in chronic phase, confirmed according to WHO 2022 diagnostic criteria. Eligible studies evaluated asciminib at any licensed dose as monotherapy compared with an approved tyrosine kinase inhibitor, placebo, or best supportive care, and reported at least one pre-specified efficacy or safety endpoint. A minimum follow-up of 12 weeks was required. We excluded case reports, case series with fewer than 20 participants, animal studies, laboratory studies, conference abstracts without accompanying full-text publication, and duplicate reports of the same patient cohort."

**Information Sources (~80 words):**
> "We searched PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), and Web of Science Core Collection from inception through [date]. Grey literature sources included ClinicalTrials.gov, the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform, and conference proceedings from the American Society of Hematology (ASH), European Hematology Association (EHA), and American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) annual meetings."

**Study Selection (~80 words):**
> "Two independent reviewers screened titles and abstracts for eligibility, with discrepancies resolved by consensus or adjudication by a third reviewer. Full-text articles of potentially eligible studies were retrieved and assessed against the pre-specified inclusion criteria. A PRISMA 2020 flow diagram documents the selection process. Inter-rater reliability was quantified using Cohen's kappa coefficient."

### Discussion (5 paragraphs, ~1100 words)

**Paragraph 1 — Summary of main findings (~160 words):**
Open with the single most important finding stated plainly. Quantify it. Then state the secondary findings, in order of clinical importance. Do not introduce new data. End with a sentence summarizing the overall clinical picture.

**Paragraph 2 — Contextualization against prior literature (~200 words):**
Compare your primary result with 2–3 specific prior studies, naming authors, years, and absolute rates. Note concordance or discordance. Explain discordance by referencing differences in patient population, follow-up duration, response definitions, or dose. This paragraph demonstrates mastery of the field.

**Paragraph 3 — Mechanistic/biological interpretation (~180 words):**
Explain WHY the findings make sense biologically or pharmacologically. Reference mechanism of action, resistance patterns, pharmacokinetics, or biomarker data. This is the paragraph that separates a data-reporting paper from a scientifically insightful one.

**Paragraph 4 — Limitations (~160 words):**
Open with the designated limitation phrase: "Interpretation of these results should be tempered by several important limitations." Enumerate 3–4 specific limitations with brief explanations of their directional impact on the results (e.g., "which may have biased response rates in favor of the intervention"). End with: what the limitations mean for clinical practice.

**Paragraph 5 — Future directions and conclusion (~160 words):**
State 2–3 specific unanswered questions that this study raises. Reference ongoing trials by name or NCT number if known. Conclude with a plain-language statement of clinical implication — what a hematologist should do differently based on this evidence.

## Anti-Bullet Conversion Rules

These sections are NEVER formatted as bullet lists in manuscript body:

| Section | Wrong Format | Required Format |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Objectives | Numbered list | Prose paragraph ending with sentence stating aim |
| PICO elements | Bold-labeled bullets | Single prose sentence per element woven into Introduction para 4 |
| Inclusion/Exclusion criteria | Italicized sub-bullets | Single prose paragraph per criterion group |
| Database list | Bullet list | "We searched X, Y, and Z from [date] through [date]" |
| Outcome list | Numbered sub-bullets | "The primary outcome was X. Secondary outcomes included Y, Z, and W." |
| Limitations | Numbered list | Prose paragraph with numbered transition if needed |

**PICO Prose Conversion Pattern:**
```
BAD:
Population: Adults with CML-CP
Intervention: Asciminib at any dose
Comparison: Standard TKIs
Outcome: MMR at 12 months

GOOD:
This systematic review evaluated the efficacy and safety of asciminib at any approved dose
in adults with Philadelphia chromosome-positive chronic myeloid leukemia in chronic phase,
compared with standard tyrosine kinase inhibitors (imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib,
or ponatinib), placebo, or best available care, with major molecular response at 12 months
as the primary outcome and complete cytogenetic response, progression-free survival,
treatment-free remission, and adverse event rates as secondary outcomes.
```

## Sentence Variety Patterns

Monotonous AI text uses the same sentence length repeatedly. Vary sentence length and opening words within every paragraph.

**Sentence length distribution (per paragraph):**
- 1–2 short sentences (8–14 words): declarative anchors
- 2–3 medium sentences (15–25 words): evidence/data sentences
- 1–2 long sentences (26–40 words): elaboration with subordinate clauses

**Opening word variety — avoid starting 2 consecutive sentences with the same word:**

| Instead of repeating | Use alternatives |
|---------------------|-----------------|
| "The..." | "These findings...", "This observation...", "Such results..." |
| "In..." | "Among patients...", "Across studies...", "Within this cohort..." |
| "Furthermore..." | "Of note,", "Importantly,", "Taken together,", "In parallel," |
| "This..." | "These data...", "Such evidence...", "The observed..." |

## Expansion Triggers (Mandatory Elaboration)

Any time these phrases appear in a draft, the paragraph is incomplete — each trigger REQUIRES an additional elaboration sentence:

| Trigger phrase | Required follow-up |
|---------------|-------------------|
| `p=X` or `p<X` | Add clinical significance of the magnitude, not just the p-value |
| `95% CI [X, Y]` | Comment on whether CI excludes clinically meaningful harm/benefit |
| `compared with [prior study]` | State the specific values from the prior study for direct comparison |
| `consistent with` | Name the specific concordant data point |
| `in contrast to` | Explain why the discordance may have arisen |
| `limitations include` | State directional impact of each limitation (over/underestimate) |
| `further research is needed` | Specify one concrete study design or population |
| `[drug] was well tolerated` | Quantify: grade ≥3 AE rate, discontinuation rate |

## Positive Humanization: What to Add

Part 13 in advanced-workflows.md covers what to REMOVE. This section covers what to ADD for text that reads as written by a senior hematologist.

### 1. Historical anchoring
Place every clinical development in a temporal frame:
> BAD: "Imatinib is the standard first-line agent for CML."
> GOOD: "Since its regulatory approval in 2001, imatinib has remained the most widely used frontline agent for CML, transforming a once-fatal disease into a manageable chronic condition for the majority of patients."

### 2. Mechanistic interpretation after every key result
> BAD: "Grade ≥3 neutropenia occurred in 20% of patients."
> GOOD: "Grade ≥3 neutropenia occurred in 20% of patients, a rate consistent with on-target myelosuppression from BCR::ABL1 kinase inhibition and comparable to that reported with bosutinib in the third-line setting."

### 3. Comparative specificity (name authors and numbers)
> BAD: "Previous studies reported similar response rates."
> GOOD: "These findings are broadly consistent with those of Hochhaus et al. (2020), who reported MMR rates of 62.5% at 24 months with nilotinib in the ENESTnd trial, though longer follow-up may attenuate this difference."

### 4. Clinical "so what" at end of each results paragraph
> BAD: End of paragraph after reporting data.
> GOOD: "The clinical relevance of this finding is particularly salient for patients who prioritize treatment-free remission, as current ELN 2022 recommendations require sustained deep molecular response for at least 24 months before a discontinuation attempt."

### 5. Nuanced limitation framing (directional impact)
> BAD: "This study has limitations including a lack of randomization."
> GOOD: "The absence of randomization is the principal methodological limitation and may have introduced selection bias, most likely inflating estimated response rates given the tendency to assign more treatment-experienced patients to novel agents in real-world practice."

### 6. Paragraph-level cohesion (last sentence of one para bridges to next)
> "Whether these molecular response advantages translate into long-term outcomes relevant to patients, including treatment-free remission eligibility and progression to advanced phase, is the question addressed by the survival analyses described below."
